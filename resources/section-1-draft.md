# SECTION 1: "THE INDUSTRY BROKE" - REVISED DRAFT

**[3 minutes. No slides. Eye contact only.]**

Let me start with a question.

**What were the greatest venture investments ever made?**

[BEAT]

In 1957, eight scientists walked out of Shockley Semiconductor. Their boss was brilliant but impossible. They had no business plan, no pitch deck, no TAM slide. Arthur Rock - an investment banker, not a VC, because the job did not exist yet - raised $1.38 million from Fairchild Camera to back them.

That single investment created the semiconductor industry. Fairchild alumni went on to found Intel, AMD, National Semiconductor - over 130 companies. Trillions of dollars in market value. The entire digital economy traces back to eight people leaving a bad job.

*[Source: Fairchild "family tree" documented by Computer History Museum; $1.38M from Lecuyer, "Making Silicon Valley"]*

There was no category called "semiconductors" when Rock wrote that check. No Gartner report. No comparable. He backed people solving a problem no one else understood yet.

[BEAT]

Same pattern, over and over.

Georges Doriot put $70,000 into DEC in 1957. Minicomputers. Everyone said only mainframes mattered. That $70K became $355 million - a 5,000x return. The best venture investment of its era.

Kleiner Perkins backed Genentech in 1976. Genetic engineering. Literal science fiction at the time. Bob Swanson cold-called Herbert Boyer and asked for ten minutes. Boyer gave him three hours. The company went public four years later at the largest IPO in history at that point.

Mike Markkula drove to a garage in Cupertino and wrote a $250,000 check for a third of Apple. No IC committee. No consensus. A personal decision based on two kids and a circuit board.

*[Source: DEC - Ante, "Creative Capital"; Genentech - Hughes, "Genentech: The Beginnings of Biotech"; Apple - Isaacson, "Steve Jobs"]*

[BEAT]

**Every legendary venture return came from the same place: one person's conviction about something most people could not yet evaluate.**

Not a playbook. Not a category. Not consensus.

Serendipity. Curiosity. Courage to fund what you cannot benchmark.

[BEAT]

Now look at what happened to the industry those investments created.

**What happens when an entire asset class converges on the same playbook?**

In 2024, the top 30 venture firms captured 75% of all US venture capital. The top 9 captured nearly half.
Everyone else - over 3,400 firms - split the remaining 25%.

In 2025 it got worse. A dozen firms raised over half of all venture capital. 1/3 of global funding went into just 68 mega-rounds.

*[Source: VC Cafe / PitchBook-NVCA 2024 data; Crunchbase 2025 data]*

Arthur Rock had no playbook. Today the entire industry runs on one.

A friend who runs allocations at a US endowment told me: "We basically take venture risk for PE returns."

MIT research published in 2024 found that higher disagreement among judges evaluating startups correlates with better outcomes. Unique ideas spark disagreement. Common ideas spark consensus. The industry is concentrating capital into the ideas that generate the most agreement. That is the opposite of what the data says works.

*[Source: "Disagreement Predicts Startup Success" - Strategy Science, 2024. Data from 67 venture competitions.]*

[BEAT]

When capital concentrates, consensus grows.
When consensus grows, pricing inflates.
When pricing inflates, returns compress.

Rock, Doriot, Markkula - they did not follow the herd. They could not. The herd did not exist yet.

The question is - does today's concentration open opportunities for investors willing to operate the way venture was originally done?

**[TRANSITION]**

I think it creates one of the most interesting structural opportunities in a generation.
