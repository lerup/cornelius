## PART 1: Mental Health Toolkit

Source: Mental Health Toolkit - Tools to Bolster Your Mood and Mental Health

---

### The Big 6 Core Pillars

These are **necessary but not sufficient** - must be maintained every 24 hours.

---

### Pillar 1: Sleep

- **Duration:** 6-8 hours per night (most adults). Some need 5, some need 9-10.
- **Consistency rule:** Go to sleep and wake up within **plus or minus 1 hour** of your regular time. Deviating more than 1 hour in either direction causes grogginess, mood dysregulation, and energy crashes - even if total sleep hours are adequate.
- **Mindset:** Treat sleep like fitness - a lifelong daily investment, not a one-time fix. No 10-week program transforms sleep forever.
- **If you miss a night:** Don't stress. Get back on schedule the next night.

---

### Pillar 2: Light / Dark

**Morning light protocol:**
- View sunlight with your eyes as early as possible after waking
- **Duration:** ~10 minutes on clear days, 20-30 minutes on overcast days
- Face east, remove sunglasses
- Do NOT view through windows or windshields (they filter relevant wavelengths)
- Glasses/contacts are fine (they focus light to retina)
- If sun is too bright to look at directly, offset gaze 10-20 degrees to the side
- **If no sunlight available:** Use a SAD lamp rated at 10,000 lux, or a 900 lux drawing tablet on your desk

**Daytime light protocol:**
- Get as much bright light (ideally sunlight) throughout the day as safely possible
- Go outside during lunch breaks, walks, etc.
- If indoors all day, keep lights as bright as safely possible

**Afternoon/evening light protocol:**
- Also view sunlight in the late afternoon/evening - critical for circadian clock regulation

**Nighttime darkness protocol (independent of sleep):**
- Be in very dim to completely dark environments for a **continuous 6-8 hours** every 24-hour cycle
- This window should fall approximately 16-24 hours after your wake-up time (i.e., your nighttime hours)
- Example: If you wake at 6 AM, be in dim/dark from ~10 PM to 6 AM
- Even small amounts of light while sleeping with eyes closed disrupts morning glucose levels
- **Key finding:** Darkness benefits mood/mental health **independently** of sleep and independently of daytime light exposure (Nature Mental Health study, 85,000+ subjects)

---

### Pillar 3: Movement / Exercise

**Cardiovascular training:**
- **Zone 2 cardio:** 180-220 minutes per week (conversation pace; if pushed harder, you couldn't talk)
- **VO2 max work:** Get heart rate very high at least 1x per week (running, cycling, swimming, Pilates, etc.)

**Resistance training:**
- 6-10 sets per muscle group, close to or at failure
- Can use weights, bands, or machines
- Split cardio and resistance to separate days

**Schedule:**
- Daily movement (either cardio or resistance)
- At least 1 full rest day per week (some need 2)

---

### Pillar 4: Nutrition

- Get sufficient but not excess quality calories daily
- Majority from non-processed or minimally processed foods (foods that perish, not shelf-stable packaged foods)
- Ensure adequate macronutrients (protein, fats, carbs) AND micronutrients (vitamins, minerals)
- Get enough probiotics, prebiotics, and fiber to support gut microbiome
- No specific diet prescribed (works with omnivore, vegan, carnivore, keto, intermittent fasting, etc.)
- **Avoid energy toxicity** (overeating) - negative for body composition AND mental health

---

### Pillar 5: Social Connection

**Audit your interactions using the Savings / Neutral / Taxing framework (Lisa Feldman Barrett):**

- **Taxing interactions:** People/groups that drain energy, cause rumination, disrupt sleep, elevate stress. Actively limit these.
- **Neutral interactions:** Neither drain nor energize. Fine to maintain.
- **Savings interactions:** People/groups that give you energy, metabolic and neurochemical resources that persist AFTER the interaction. Prioritize these.

**Protocol:**
- Spend 5-10 minutes identifying which people/groups fall into each category
- Notice how much of your internal dialogue (even when alone) is occupied by taxing interactions
- Consciously shift time toward savings interactions

---

### Pillar 6: Stress Control

**Tool 1 - Real-time stress reduction: Physiological Sigh**

For immediate calming in any stressful moment:

1. Deep inhale through the nose (maximize lung inflation)
2. Before exhaling, sneak in a second brief inhale through the nose (to maximally inflate lungs and open collapsed alveoli)
3. Long extended exhale through the mouth (to lungs empty)
4. Typically just 1 repetition is sufficient. Sometimes 2-3.

- Works the first time and every time
- Based on a hardwired neural circuit (discovered 1930s)
- If deviated septum: do first two inhales through nose, exhale through mouth

**Tool 2 - Raise your stress threshold: Deliberate Cold Exposure**

To build capacity to stay calm under stress:

1. Get into a cold shower (or cold plunge)
2. Stay for ~1 minute
3. Your adrenaline will spike - this is non-negotiable, even for experienced users
4. Practice controlling your breathing and staying calm while adrenaline is elevated
5. Frequency: 1-7x per week, minimum 1x per week

- Purpose: Practice maintaining clear cognition under elevated adrenaline
- Analogy: Like learning to drive in fog - builds comfort in extreme conditions

---

### Cyclic Physiological Sighing Protocol (5 min/day)

Published clinical trial (Cell Reports Medicine, Huberman & Spiegel labs):

1. Set a timer for 5 minutes
2. Sit or lie down (any time of day)
3. Repeat the physiological sigh continuously for 5 minutes:
   - Double inhale through nose (big inhale + small top-off inhale)
   - Long exhale through mouth to lungs empty
   - Repeat immediately
4. Do this daily

**Outcomes (from clinical trial):**
- Pervasive mood improvement (around the clock, not just during practice)
- Reduced anxiety
- Improved sleep metrics
- Improved heart rate variability
- Improved autonomic function

---

### Emotional Granularity Protocol (Lisa Feldman Barrett)

**Protocol: Emotional check-ins 3-6x per day**

1. Set periodic reminders OR use natural triggers (every time you enter an elevator, climb stairs, etc.)
2. Ask yourself: "What am I really feeling right now?"
3. **Avoid broad labels:** Do NOT use "good," "okay," "bad," "fine"
4. **Use specific compound descriptions:** "curious but slightly anxious," "energized and happy," "fatigued and confused," "bored but in positive anticipation about tomorrow"
5. You don't need to write it down (though you can)
6. You don't need to say it aloud - internal labeling works

**Outcomes:**
- Enhances positive emotional experiences
- Improves ability to navigate negative emotions (negative emotions carry useful information)
- Correlates with improved vagal tone and heart rate variability
- Improves respiratory sinus arrhythmia
- Effects are pervasive (24-hour improvement, not just during practice)

### Self-Concept Building: Life Narrative Exercise (Paul Conti)

**Setup:**
1. Create a main folder (physical or digital) titled "Lifetime"
2. Inside, create sub-folders for each 3-5 year increment of your life (e.g., 0-5, 6-10, 11-15, 16-20, etc.)
3. Extend folders to ~2 years beyond your current age

**For each folder:**
1. Create a single document
2. Add bullet points (not journal entries) describing key milestone events from that period
3. Include: where you lived, school/work, influential people, key experiences (positive, negative, neutral), transformative moments, relationships, hobbies, things you're processing from earlier periods
4. **Inclusion test:** If you want to make sure you never forget it, or it keeps surfacing in your mind - include it
5. Continue adding to the current-age folder as life happens

**Purpose:**
- Builds structured narrative representation of your life
- Anchors perception of time passage
- Reveals behavioral patterns (stuck in loops or progressing)
- Foundation for deeper self-exploration work
- NOT goal-setting - purely retrospective understanding

### Dream Journaling Protocol (Paul Conti)

**Setup:** Keep a journal (paper + pen) beside your bed.

**Upon waking:**
1. Keep your body completely still and eyes closed
2. Stay still for 1-3 minutes (up to 5) to let dream content surface
3. Write down key bullet points from the dream (or full narrative if you can)
4. If dream content arrives later in the day, write it down then

**Analysis method:**
- Do NOT interpret individual dreams
- Track recurring **themes** across multiple dreams
- Look for themes that repeat in different forms across different nights
- These recurring themes = your unconscious mind's active concerns

---

### Liminal State Exploration (Paul Conti)

**When:** Immediately upon waking, before getting out of bed

1. Keep eyes closed and body completely still
2. Remain in this state for 1-5 minutes
3. Simply observe where your mind goes - do not direct it
4. Notice: Does it go to work? Relationships? Self? Anxiety? Desires?
5. Write down what surfaced

**Purpose:** Access unconscious mind content without formal dream analysis. Easier than dream recall for most people.

---

### Journaling Protocols (Paul Conti)

**Two distinct modes - use both:**

**Mode 1: Free Association / Data Dump**
- Duration: 5-10 minutes (up to 30 if you have time)
- Write anything that comes to mind
- No structure, no editing, no concern about handwriting or quality
- Best done in the morning
- Purpose: Clear mental clutter, surface unconscious concerns
- **Critical:** This is for your eyes only. If you self-monitor ("how will this look?"), it defeats the purpose.

**Mode 2: Structured Goals & Aspirations Journaling**
- Set a specific topic before writing: goals, aspirations, what you want to create
- Include: material goals, feeling-state goals ("I want to feel part of a community"), contribution goals
- Flesh out detail: size, scope, timeline, who's involved, where, how
- Can address past desires, present wants, future aspirations
- Frequency: daily, 3x/week, or even 1x/week - no strict rules

**Note:** Many people resist structured goal journaling due to fear of disappointment. The resistance itself is a signal to do the exercise. Not thinking about goals does not protect you - it prevents actualization.

---

### Trauma Processing Language Rule (Paul Conti)

**Core principle:** Allow language as big as the trauma itself.

- Do NOT minimize language when describing traumas (written or spoken)
- Do NOT use passive voice or diminishing qualifiers
- Do NOT distract yourself from processing traumas
- Suppressed traumas root into the unconscious and manifest as: waking at 2-3 AM with racing thoughts, compulsive/obsessive thinking, overwork as avoidance, substance overuse

**What to do:**
- Use language that captures the actual magnitude of impact
- Talk about it with trusted people
- Write about it with full emotional intensity
- Major or repeated traumas: work with a licensed clinician (psychologist or psychiatrist)

**Recommended resource:** "The Invisible Epidemic" by Dr. Paul Conti

---

### Supplement Mentions for Mood (not primary recommendations)

- **L-Tyrosine:** 1-3 grams daily (amino acid precursor to dopamine)
- **Alpha-GPC:** 300-600 mg daily (enhances acetylcholine)
- **L-Tryptophan:** precursor to serotonin (no specific dose given)

Note: These have less potency than prescription medications. No clinical trials yet confirm their neuroplasticity effects. Mentioned as what some people use, not as primary protocol.

---

### Mental Health Defined (Paul Conti)

**States to aspire to:** Agency and Gratitude (as active verb states, not passive feelings)

**What emerges from agency + gratitude:** Peace, Contentment, Delight

**The three drives:**
- **Aggressive drive:** Determination, leaning into friction (on a continuum)
- **Pleasure drive:** Desire for pleasure (on a continuum from hedonism to self-restriction)
- **Generative drive:** "Our desire to create, build, and contribute to the world in meaningful ways and appreciate the process to get there. It is the core feature of our mental health."

**Key rule:** Aggressive drive and pleasure drive must never outsize the generative drive.

## PART 2: Goals Toolkit

Source: Goals Toolkit - How to Set and Achieve Your Goals

---

### Goal Difficulty Calibration

- **Too easy:** Doesn't recruit enough autonomic nervous system arousal. Low motivation.
- **Too hard / impossible:** Crashes blood pressure system. Even if mentally exciting, body doesn't enter readiness state. Leads to feeling unmotivated.
- **Optimal: Moderately challenging.** Just outside current abilities. "That would take a lot of effort but it's within range - maybe I can do it, maybe I can't."
- Setting moderate goals produces a **near doubling** of the likelihood of sustained pursuit.

---

### Visualization Protocol

**What works:**
- **Visualize failure** (primary method) - think specifically about how bad it will be if you don't achieve the goal. How disappointing. How it will negatively impact you.
- The more specific and vivid the failure visualization, the more effective
- Near **doubling in probability** of reaching goals when routinely foreshadowing failure

**What doesn't work well:**
- Visualizing success / the big win (Super Bowl, graduation, dream partner, etc.)
- Effective only for **initiating** goal pursuit. Poor for **maintaining** it. Can be counterproductive.
- Use positive visualization only at the very beginning, then occasionally

---

### Visual Focus Protocol for Goal Pursuit

**Pre-action activation (30-60 seconds):**

1. Pick a single point beyond your immediate reach (computer screen, wall, horizon)
2. Focus your visual attention on that one point for 30-60 seconds
3. Don't move your head. Blinking is fine.
4. This engages: increased blood pressure, adrenaline release, dopamine recruitment, alert/ready state
5. Then immediately begin the actions that move you toward your goal

**Why it works:**
- Focusing on a goal line = 17% less perceived effort and 23% faster completion (Balcetis lab, NYU)
- Narrow visual focus activates goal-directed neural circuits
- Diffuse visual attention (looking at everything) deactivates goal pursuit

---

### Milestone & Reward Schedule

**Milestone structure:**
- Do NOT only focus on the finish line
- Set intermediate milestones between now and the ultimate goal
- Milestones should be spaced at intervals you define in advance

**Progress assessment:**
- Check in at the end of each week
- Look back on the previous week
- Assess: How well did you perform? How many times did you do/avoid the target behavior?
- Weekly assessment reups dopamine (motivation chemical) and confirms you're on track

**Reward timing:**
- **Intermittent and random** reward is the most effective reinforcement schedule known
- Do NOT reward yourself on a fixed schedule after every milestone
- Let some rewards come at unexpected intervals
- This maps to dopamine reward prediction error: unexpected positive events produce the largest dopamine spikes

---

### Dopamine Management During Goal Pursuit

**Reward Prediction Error - how to use it:**

- **Largest dopamine hit:** Something positive happens that you did NOT expect
- **Medium dopamine:** You anticipate something good, then it happens (dopamine during anticipation + smaller hit at delivery)
- **Dopamine crash (disappointment):** You expect something good and it doesn't happen - dopamine drops BELOW baseline

**Practical rules:**
- Don't over-anticipate rewards (you'll get less dopamine when they arrive)
- Build in some unpredictability to your reward structure
- Weekly check-ins reup dopamine by confirming progress
- Behavioral tools (visual focus, etc.) build plasticity over time - you get BETTER at motivation and focus through practice

---

### Space-Time Bridging Practice

**Duration:** 90 seconds to 3 minutes. Daily or semi-daily.

**Steps (work through each "station"):**

**Station 1 - Internal (interoception):**
- Close eyes
- Focus all attention internally: breathing, heart rate, skin sensation
- 3 slow breaths
- Attention split: 100% internal

**Station 2 - Body surface (transition):**
- Open eyes
- Focus visual attention on a point on your own body (e.g., palm of hand)
- 3 breaths
- Attention split: ~90% internal, ~10% external

**Station 3 - Near external:**
- Shift visual attention to an object 5-15 feet away
- 3 breaths
- Attention split: ~90% external, ~10% internal (maintain breath awareness)

**Station 4 - Far external (horizon):**
- Shift visual attention to the farthest point you can see (horizon, distant wall)
- 3 breaths
- Attention split: ~99-100% external

**Station 5 - Panoramic vision:**
- Expand field of view as wide as possible (peripheral vision, full visual landscape)
- Don't focus on any single point
- 3 breaths

**Station 6 - Return:**
- Close eyes
- Return to 100% internal focus
- 3 breaths

**Repeat the full sequence 2-3 times.**

**Purpose:** Trains your visual-cognitive system to move flexibly between immediate experience and long-term goal orientation. Close focus = fine time-slicing (seconds, heartbeats). Far focus = broad time-batching (days, weeks, months). This flexibility is the essence of goal-directed behavior.

---

### Summary: Goal Achievement Protocol (Step by Step)

1. **Define the goal:** Must be moderately challenging - just outside current abilities but within plausible range
2. **Set milestones:** Break the gap between now and the goal into intermediate checkpoints
3. **Define action steps:** Concrete, specific actions (go/no-go decisions)
4. **Visualize failure:** Routinely think about how bad it will be if you don't achieve the goal (primary motivator)
5. **Visualize success sparingly:** Only at the very start and occasionally thereafter
6. **Pre-action visual focus:** Before each work session, focus eyes on a single point for 30-60 seconds to activate pursuit state
7. **Weekly assessment:** Every week, review progress toward milestones. This reups dopamine.
8. **Random intermittent rewards:** Don't reward on a fixed schedule. Let some rewards be unexpected.
9. **Practice space-time bridging:** Daily or semi-daily, 90 seconds to 3 minutes, to build flexible goal-orientation capacity
10. **Use behavioral tools before supplements:** Behavioral tools build lasting neuroplasticity. Supplements (caffeine, L-tyrosine) can support but don't build the same long-term capacity.
